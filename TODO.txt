Question / answers (from scholarly evidence):
- Fentanyl vs opioids generally: There IS a real, evidence-based clinical
  difference, but it is in the ACUTE phase, not the long-term recovery
  timeline. Fentanyl is highly lipophilic and accumulates in adipose tissue,
  creating a slow-release reservoir that prolongs withdrawal vulnerability and
  makes buprenorphine induction prone to precipitated withdrawal (clearance is
  far longer than the ~2-4 days of other short-acting opioids). The months-long
  recovery arc (dopamine/reward, sleep, immune, cognition) is the same as
  general opioids. Conclusion: no separate fentanyl ENTRY needed for milestone
  purposes; the distinction belongs in acute-withdrawal guidance, not the
  recovery timeline. (Refs: PMC7594258; fentanyl ADME review PMC10593981.)
- Chewing/smokeless tobacco: Nicotine withdrawal itself is the same as our
  other nicotine entries. The differentiator would be health-recovery harms,
  which are oral (gum disease, oral/oesophageal cancer) rather than pulmonary —
  so it does NOT share smoking's lung/cardio-via-smoke milestones and overlaps
  heavily with nicotine pouches. Verdict: defensible but low marginal value; if
  added, model it on nicotine pouches with oral-health milestones, not smoking.
  Our existing split (smoking vs vaping vs pouches) is justified mainly by
  differing delivery/health-recovery profiles, less by withdrawal.
- Codeine vs opioids: No separate entry. Codeine withdrawal follows the general
  opioid pattern; the real differences are patient population and combination-
  product toxicity (acetaminophen/aspirin), not the recovery trajectory. Keep
  under Opioids. (Refs: WHO NBK310652; case series PMID 25545541.)
